AI & Global Logistics

Economic Transformation by 2030

by Thien Nhan Nguyen – S3972179

As agentic AI systems mature and adoption scales beyond pilots by 2030, AI will reshape global logistics by reducing uncertainty and lowering coordination costs across supply networks, although these benefits will be distributed unevenly.

Thesis Statement

Global logistics networks underpin modern trade, but persistent forecasting errors, inventory misallocation, and transport delays continue to impose significant costs on firms and consumers through higher operating expenses, disrupted production, and inflationary pressure.

This forecast argues that, as agentic systems mature and adoption scales beyond pilots by 2030, AI will reshape global logistics by reducing uncertainty and lowering coordination costs across supply networks, although these benefits will be distributed unevenly. Forecasting and optimisation can cut logistics costs by 5 to 20 per cent (Oca et al. 2024), yet these gains compound with data scale, favouring hyperscale platforms and large logistics providers over smaller suppliers. As a general-purpose technology, AI extends beyond physical automation into cognitive tasks such as demand forecasting, route optimisation, and operational management. In doing so, it shifts competitive advantage away from labour-cost arbitrage and towards data-driven coordination. While some efficiency gains may reach consumers through lower prices and improved services, the largest share is likely to be captured by data-rich firms, while routine logistics workers and developing economies face greater displacement risk. Consequently, AI's economic impact will depend on policy choices surrounding competition, workforce transition, and data governance.

Core Capabilities

Predictive Analytics and Firm-Level Concentration

AI enables predictive analytics by processing large volumes of real-time data into accurate forecasts, reducing the uncertainty that forces firms to hold costly safety stock and over-provision capacity. As forecasts tighten, firms run leaner inventories, suffer fewer stockouts, and increase vehicle and warehouse utilisation, which can lower logistics costs by 5 to 20 percent and inventory by 20 to 30 percent (Oca et al., 2024).

However, forecasting accuracy depends on data scale, where firms with the largest datasets can train the better models and win more business, creating a self-reinforcing flywheel. As agentic systems become deeply involved in planning and execution across the chain by 2030, this process intensifies the divergence at the firm level, where value gains concentrate among hyperscalers and established firms.

Warehouse Automation, Labour Displacement and the Productivity-Wage Gap

AI-powered robotics and computer vision systems are increasingly automating handling activities, allowing warehouses to process higher volumes with fewer errors. Amazon expects to automate up to 75 percent of its operations by 2033, while routine occupations in warehousing and logistics face increasing automation pressure.

Although automation creates new job opportunities in systems maintenance and engineering, it can displace workers from existing tasks faster than new tasks are reinstated. As machines handle routine tasks more efficiently and firms become less dependent on routine labour, workers' bargaining power weakens. A larger share of productivity gains is therefore retained as profits rather than distributed through wages.

Route Optimisation and Global Supply Chain Coordination

AI-driven coordination systems analyse traffic conditions, weather disruptions, supplier performance, and delivery schedules to improve decision-making across supply networks. By making complex networks cheaper to coordinate, AI reshapes firms' decisions about what to make in-house and where to locate production.

Although cheaper coordination alone could support further offshoring, automation allows machines to substitute for production labour, reducing cost savings from low-wage locations. As a result, firms become less geographically dependent on cheap labour, favouring regionalisation and reshoring. Approximately two million manufacturing jobs have returned to the United States since 2010, with automation identified as a key driver.

Baseline: AI-Augmented Logistics

Key Outcome: Moderately more productive and more concentrated industry with gradual widening of inequalities.

At the Firm Level

AI-enabled predictive analytics, warehouse automation, and route optimisation become standard operational tools across the logistics sector, yet value remains concentrated among hyperscalers. Diffusion lags as data readiness, integration, and skills shortages continue to constrain adoption, resulting in productivity gains emerging gradually rather than through sudden disruption.

At the Labour Level

Displacement is partial and uneven, cushioned by persistent labour shortages that absorb some workers released by automation. As routine roles decline and technical roles grow, governments implement moderate retraining initiatives that help some displaced workers transition.

At the Global Level

Regionalisation proceeds steadily as coordination costs fall and automation reduces dependence on labour-cost arbitrage; the offshore base erodes gradually rather than collapsing, while policy remains reactive rather than anticipatory.

Upside: Autonomous Supply Networks

Key Outcome: AI becomes a source of widespread prosperity with accessible technology, full employment, and developing-economy participation.

At the Firm Level

Advances in agentic AI systems combine with wider access to data, cloud infrastructure, and affordable automation tools, lowering adoption barriers for small- and mid-sized firms. Competition and interoperability rules keep markets contestable, allowing smaller firms to compete rather than merely adapt.

At the Labour Level

Governments successfully manage labour-market transition through large-scale investment in reskilling programmes. New occupations emerge quickly enough to absorb displaced workers into higher-value roles, preventing large-scale unemployment and limiting the widening of the productivity–wage gap.

At the Global Level

Developing economies gain access to the same tools as advanced economies, allowing them to move up the value chain rather than simply losing their cost advantage. This creates a more productive and resilient logistics system while consumers benefit from lower prices, faster delivery and greater product availability.

Downside: Concentrated and Fragile Logistics

Key Outcome: Technological success but significant economic and social challenges with concentrated gains and widespread displacement.

At the Firm Level

Data scale becomes the critical source of competitive advantage, pushing the concentration channel to its extreme. Incumbents capture most productivity gains and increase barriers to entry as smaller firms struggle to replicate the datasets, computing infrastructure, and capital investment required to compete. This results in a winner-take-most market.

At the Labour Level

Displacement accelerates as automation displaces routine workers faster than new roles emerge. Labour's wage share falls sharply and inequality widens. More than 92 million roles could be displaced by emerging technologies by 2030, highlighting the scale of disruption if transition policies fail to keep pace.

At the Global Level

Developing economies that relied on labour-cost arbitrage experience declining participation in global supply chains, deepening economic divergence between advanced and developing economies. Logistics networks become more productive but also more concentrated, widening inequality between dominant platforms and smaller firms.

Policy Playbook

Three strategic policy levers to shape the distribution of AI gains in logistics:

1. Competition and Data Access Policy

Responsibility: ACCC, European Commission, data-protection authorities, governments, major platforms

When: Early / pre-emptive, before data lock-in becomes entrenched

Because AI performance relies on data scale, large firms can leverage this advantage to capture markets while creating barriers to entry for smaller competitors. Government should treat data scale as a source of market power and restore contestability through interoperability and portability requirements. Regulators must act decisively before lock-in becomes entrenched, while carefully calibrating rules so they do not deter investment and innovation.

2. Workforce Transition and Reskilling Programmes

Responsibility: Governments, Department of Employment, TAFE, universities, firms, unions

When: Before and during automation rollout

As AI automation raises productivity by substituting for routine warehouse and logistics tasks, workers are displaced faster than new technical roles are created. Reskilling programmes should be sector-specific, targeted at workers most exposed to automation, and linked to real jobs in robotics maintenance, data analytics, systems supervision, and logistics coordination. Reskilling must be paired with wage insurance and labour protection and deployed before displacement accelerates.

3. Global Adjustment and Inclusive Supply Chains

Responsibility: WTO, UNCTAD, World Bank, development-finance institutions, governments

When: Early and long-term, before global divergence becomes entrenched

As competitiveness shifts from cheap labour towards data, technology, and capital, developing economies that relied on labour-cost arbitrage face exclusion rather than simple adjustment. Policy must prevent developing economies from being locked out of AI-enabled trade participation through technology transfer, development finance, and fair trade rules. Advanced economies must commit to joint cross-border initiatives that create mutual benefits.

Methods & Key Insights

Forecast Approach

This forecast uses a scenario-based approach examining how AI may reshape global logistics by 2030, drawing on peer-reviewed economic theory including the task model of automation, transaction-cost economics, the economics of data, and global value-chain research, combined with industry and institutional sources.

Central Assumption

AI adoption continues to expand from pilot projects into operational systems, particularly through predictive analytics, warehouse automation, route optimisation, and autonomous coordination tools. The forecast is structured around three causal channels: firm-level productivity and concentration, labour-market displacement, and supply-chain geography.

Key Finding

AI will make logistics more productive by 2030, but productivity alone does not guarantee broad prosperity. Without policy intervention, gains will concentrate among data-rich firms, capital owners, and advanced economies. The central challenge is whether institutions can make those gains contestable, inclusive, and resilient.
